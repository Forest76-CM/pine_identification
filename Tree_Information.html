Common Pine Species Information

Colorado Pines

Pinus contorta - lodgepole pine

Leaves

  • Acicular, fasciles of 2
  • 1.25-3 inches, persistent 4.6 years
  • Dark green to yellow-green

Cones

  • 0.75-2 inches, subcylindric to ovoid
  • Tawny to dark brown; knob-like scales
  • Often serotinous: dorsal umbo armed

Twigs

  • Stout; buds slightly resinous

Bark

  • Coastal: reddish-brown to black, thick
  • Mountain: orange-brown to gray, thin

Other Characteristics

  • Elevation: 1500-11, 500 ft
  • Shade intolerant; typically seral
  • Associates: Douglas-fir, ponderosa pine, subalpine fir, limber pine
Pinus ponderosa - ponderosa pine

Leaves

  • Acicular, fascicles of 2-3 (5)
  • 4-8 inches,persistent 3 years
  • Yellow-green to gray-green

Cones

  • 2-6 inches, ovoid to ellipsoidal
  • Reddish-brown to brownish-yellow
  • Sessile or short-stalked; dorsal umbo armed

Twigs

  • Stout; buds with resin drops

Bark

  • Young: brown-black, deeply furrowed
  • Old: yellowish-brown to cinnamon-red, flat plates

Other Characteristics

  • Elevation: 4500-8500 ft (Rocky Mtns)
  • Shade intolerant; clima or seral
  • Occurs in pure or mixed stands
Pinus flexilis - limber pine

Leaves

  • Acicular, fascicles of 5
  • 2-3.5 inches, persistent
  • Dark green, clustered at ends,/li>

Cones

  • 3-6 (10) inches, cylindric to ovoid
  • Light yellow-brown; stiff scales
  • Terminal umbo; unarmed
  • Wingless seeds; large cavity

Bark

  • Young: silvery-white to greenish-gray
  • Old: dark brown-black; rectangular plates

Other Characteristics

  • Elevation: 4000-11, 500 ft
  • Dry, rocky slopes; drooping branches
  • Small tree (30-50 ft)
  • Low competition tolerance; seral
Pinus aristata — bristlecone pine

Leaves

  • Acicular, fascicles of 5
  • 1–1.5 inches; resin flecks

Cones

  • 2–3 inches, ovoid
  • Bristle-tipped scales

Bark

  • Young: gray-brown
  • Old: orange-brown, scaly

Other Characteristics

  • Extremely long-lived (up to 4000+ years)
  • High-elevation, dry rocky soils
  • Shade intolerant
  • Open or pure stands

Southwest Pines

Pinus edulis – piñon pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 2 (rarely 1)
  • 1–2 inches long
  • Yellow-green; stout; persistent

Cones

  • 1.5–2 inches, ovoid
  • Light brown; thick, rounded scales
  • Dorsal umbo; unarmed
  • Large edible seeds (“pine nuts”)

Bark

  • Thin, gray-brown; irregular plates
  • Becomes scaly with age

Other Characteristics

  • Occurs on dry slopes and mesas
  • Common in pinyon–juniper woodlands
  • Small tree (20–40 ft)
  • Southwestern U.S. distribution
Pinus monophylla – singleleaf piñon

Leaves

  • Single needle per fascicle (only pine with fascicle = 1)
  • 1–2.5 inches long
  • Glaucous (bluish-green)

Cones

  • 1.5–2 inches long
  • Thick scales; reddish-brown
  • Dorsal umbo; unarmed
  • Large edible seeds

Bark

  • Thin, gray to dark-brown
  • Breaks into small plates

Other Characteristics

  • Occurs on dry slopes and basins
  • Widest-ranging pinyon species
  • Small tree (20–40 ft)

Exotic Pines

Pinus nigra – Austrian pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 2
  • 4–6 inches long
  • Dark green; very stiff

Cones

  • 2–3 inches; ovoid
  • Light brown; unarmed umbo
  • Thick, shiny scales

Bark

  • Dark gray to black when young
  • Females plates form gray–brown scaly ridges with age

Other Characteristics

  • Introduced from Europe
  • Common ornamental/urban pine
  • Tolerant of pollution and poor soils
Pinus sylvestris – Scotch pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 2
  • 1.5–3 inches
  • Twisted; blue-green

Cones

  • 1–2.5 inches; ovoid–conic
  • Light brown; unarmed umbo

Bark

  • Orange–reddish on upper trunk
  • Gray and flaky below

Other Characteristics

  • Introduced from northern Europe
  • Common Christmas-tree species
  • Adapted to cold climates

Northwest pines

Pinus albicaulis – whitebark pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 5
  • 1.5–3 inches; stout
  • Blue-green; curved

Cones

  • 2–3 inches; ovoid
  • Reddish-purple when young
  • Thick, blunt scales
  • Terminal umbo; unarmed
  • Cones do not open—seeds removed by Clark’s nutcracker

Bark

  • Thin; light gray to whitish
  • Breaks into small scales

Other Characteristics

  • High elevation: 6,500–11,000 ft
  • Wind-swept ridges & cold alpine sites
  • Often krummholz (stunted, twisted forms)
  • Sticky white sap
Pinus monticola – Western white pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 5
  • 2.5–4 inches; slender; soft
  • Blue-green

Cones

  • 5–12 inches long; cylindrical
  • Curved slightly
  • Thin, flexible scales
  • Terminal umbo; unarmed

Bark

  • Young: smooth, gray-green
  • Old: dark gray; rectangular plates

Other Characteristics

  • Medium to large tree (100–150 ft)
  • Montane forests of the Pacific Northwest
  • Vulnerable to white pine blister rust
Pinus lambertiana – sugar pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 5
  • 2.5–4 inches; flexible

Cones

  • 12–24 inches long; the longest of any pine
  • Ellipsoid-cylindric
  • Thick scales; rounded
  • Terminal umbo; unarmed

Bark

  • Thick, deeply furrowed
  • Brown to purplish-brown

Other Characteristics

  • Largest Pinus species (180–220 ft)
  • Grows primarily in California mountains
  • Sugar-rich resin exudate (“sweet pine”)
Pinus jeffreyi – Jeffrey pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 2–3
  • 5–10 inches; stout
  • Blue-green

Cones

  • 6–12 inches long
  • Dorsal umbo with inward-pointing prickles (gentler than ponderosa)
  • Large, heavy, oval-cylindric

Bark

  • Red-brown with deep furrows
  • Smells like vanilla or butterscotch when warmed

Other Characteristics

  • Occurs in CA, OR, NV mountains
  • Often confused with ponderosa (but cones differ)

California pines

Pinus attenuata – knobcone pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 3
  • 4–7 inches long; stiff
  • Yellow-green

Cones

  • 3–6 inches; strongly asymmetrical
  • Serotinous; remain closed for many years
  • Tightly clustered along the stem
  • Dorsal umbo; armed

Bark

  • Thin, gray-brown
  • Becomes scaly with age

Other Characteristics

  • Fire-adapted; cones open after intense heat
  • Dry low-elevation habitats
  • Small to medium tree
Pinus radiata – Monterey pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 3 (sometimes 2)
  • 4–6 inches; dark green; flexible

Cones

  • 3–6 inches; asymmetrical
  • Serotinous or non-serotinous depending on region
  • Thick, woody scales
  • Dorsal umbo; armed

Bark

  • Dark gray-brown
  • Deeply furrowed

Other Characteristics

  • Very limited natural range (California coast)
  • Extensively planted worldwide
  • Medium-sized tree (60–100 ft)
Pinus torreyana – Torrey pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 5
  • 8–13 inches; stout; gray-green

Cones

  • 4–6 inches long
  • Massive, heavy, rounded cones
  • Dorsal umbo; armed

Bark

  • Thick, rough, fissured
  • Dark brown

Other Characteristics

  • Rarest native pine in North America
  • Restricted to coastal San Diego & Santa Rosa Island
  • Wind-pruned coastal crown
Pinus coulteri – Coulter pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 3
  • 8–12 inches; blue-green; stiff

Cones

  • 8–14 inches long
  • Heaviest cones of any pine (“widow-makers”)
  • Massive scales with large hooked claws
  • Dorsal umbo; strongly armed

Bark

  • Thick; dark brown-gray
  • Deep ridges

Other Characteristics

  • Dry foothills & chaparral margins
  • Medium-sized tree (40–80 ft)
  • Slow-growing
Pinus sabiniana – gray pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 3
  • 8–12 inches; pale gray-green
  • Very drooping needles

Cones

  • 6–10 inches long
  • Massive, heavy cones
  • Thick scales; strongly armed umbo
  • Large edible seeds

Bark

  • Thick, dark gray to brown
  • Deep furrows

Other Characteristics

  • Hot, dry California foothills
  • Open, sparse crown
  • Medium-sized tree (40–70 ft)

Southeastern Pines

Pinus taeda – loblolly pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 3 (sometimes 2)
  • 5–9 inches; slender
  • Yellow-green

Cones

  • 2.5–6 inches; ovoid to cylindrical
  • Dorsal umbo; armed
  • Persistent for several years

Twigs

  • Stout; orange-brown
  • Large resinous buds

Bark

  • Thick, reddish-brown
  • Deep furrows with scaly plates

Other Characteristics

  • Common southeastern pine
  • Fast-growing; forms pure stands
Pinus echinata – shortleaf pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 2–3
  • 3–5 inches; slender
  • Dark yellow-green

Cones

  • 1.5–2.5 inches; ovoid
  • Dorsal umbo; armed
  • Small egg-shaped cones

Twigs

  • Slender; reddish-brown
  • Small non-resinous buds

Bark

  • Dark irregular plates
  • Pitch pockets present

Other Characteristics

  • Occurs on dry upland sites
  • Widespread southern species
Pinus palustris – longleaf pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 3
  • 8–18 inches; very long
  • Bright green; tufted

Cones

  • 6–10 inches; large and cylindrical
  • Dorsal umbo; armed
  • Thick heavy scales

Twigs

  • Thick; large white buds (“candles”)

Bark

  • Thick, orange-brown
  • Large flat plates

Other Characteristics

  • Grass stage seedlings
  • Fire-adapted dominant species
Pinus elliottii – slash pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 2–3
  • 8–12 inches
  • Dark glossy green

Cones

  • 5–8 inches; glossy brown
  • Dorsal umbo; armed
  • Often in clusters

Twigs

  • Thick; brown; resinous

Bark

  • Reddish-brown
  • Large irregular plates

Other Characteristics

  • Coastal plains species
  • Fast-growing and widely planted

Eastern Pines

Pinus banksiana – jack pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 2
  • 0.75–2 inches; twisted
  • Yellow-green

Cones

  • 1–2 inches; curved and asymmetric
  • Often serotinous
  • Dorsal umbo; armed

Twigs

  • Stout; slightly resinous buds

Bark

  • Thin; reddish-brown to gray
  • Scaly plates

Other Characteristics

  • Dry sandy soils
  • Fire-adapted species
Pinus resinosa – red pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 2
  • 4–6 inches; brittle
  • Dark green

Cones

  • 1.5–2.5 inches; ovoid
  • Dorsal umbo; unarmed

Twigs

  • Red-brown; non-resinous buds

Bark

  • Reddish scaly plates
  • Flaky with age

Other Characteristics

  • Forms pure stands
  • Distinct reddish trunk
Pinus strobus – Eastern white pine

Leaves

  • Fascicles of 5
  • 3–5 inches; soft
  • Blue-green

Cones

  • 4–8 inches; slender
  • Thin scales; terminal umbo; unarmed

Twigs

  • Slender; smooth
  • Non-resinous buds

Bark

  • Young: greenish to dark gray
  • Old: deeply furrowed

Other Characteristics

  • Large eastern tree (80–150 ft)
  • Lightweight white wood
